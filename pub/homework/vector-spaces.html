Problem 1: Classify Vector Spaces*

Classify whether each of the following is a rational vector space or not. Remember that we need to check three things: that there is a zero in VVV, that the sum of two things in VVV is still in VVV, and that all scalar multiples of anything in VVV are still in VVV.

  1. V:={0}V := \{0\}V:={0}

  2. V:=Z={…,−2,−1,0,1,2,… }V := \mathbf{Z} = \{\dots, -2, -1, 0, 1, 2, \dots\}V:=Z={…,−2,−1,0,1,2,…}

  3. V:=set of solutions to x+y=4V := \text{set of solutions to }x + y = 4V:=set of solutions to x+y=4

  4. V:=set of solutions to xy=0V := \text{set of solutions to }xy = 0V:=set of solutions to xy=0

  5. V:=set of solutions to x−y=0V := \text{set of solutions to }x - y = 0V:=set of solutions to x−y=0

  6. V:=R=set of real numbersV := \mathbf{R} = \text{set of real numbers}V:=R=set of real numbers

  7. V:=set of polynomials with rational coefficientsV := \text{set of polynomials with rational coefficients}V:=set of polynomials with rational coefficients

Solution

  1. Yes, it is, since all linear combinations of 000 are still 000.

  2. No, it is not. For example, 12⋅1\frac{1}{2} \cdot 121​⋅1 is a scalar multiple of 111 which is not in VVV.

  3. No, it is not. For example, [0,4]∈V[0, 4]\in V[0,4]∈V and [4,0]∈V[4, 0]\in V[4,0]∈V, but [0,4]+[4,0]=[4,4]∉V[0, 4] + [4, 0] = [4, 4] \notin V[0,4]+[4,0]=[4,4]∈/​V.

  4. No, it is not. For example, [0,1]∈V[0, 1]\in V[0,1]∈V and [1,0]∈V[1, 0]\in V[1,0]∈V, but [0,1]+[1,0]=[1,1]∉V[0, 1] + [1, 0] = [1, 1] \notin V[0,1]+[1,0]=[1,1]∈/​V.

  5. Yes, it is, since the solutions to a homogeneous linear equation are a vector space.

  6. Yes, it is. We can check that 000 is a real number, the sum of two real numbers is always a real number, and that scalar multiplying real numbers by rational numbers still gives you a real number.

  7. Yes, it is. We can check that 000 meets the definition of a polynomial, the sum of two polynomials with rational coefficients is always a polynomial, and that scalar multiplying polynomials with rational coefficients by rational numbers still gives you a polynomial with rational coefficients.

Problem 2: Canada Day Picnic

Gianni bought food for his planned Canada Day Picnic. He spent $100 on three kinds of food: hot dogs, hamburgers, and salad boxes. Each hot dog costs $2, each hamburger $3, and each salad box $4.

  1. Let xxx represent the number of hot dogs bought, yyy represent the number of hamburgers bought, and zzz represent the number of salad boxes bought. Write a linear equation to represent this problem.

  2. Should xxx, yyy, and zzz be allowed to be a) natural numbers, b) integers, c) rational numbers, or d) real numbers?

  3. Find three distinct solutions based on your selection above.

Solution

  1. 2x+3y+4z=1002x + 3y + 4z = 1002x+3y+4z=100

  2. a) natural numbers, because it only makes sense to buy a non-negative whole number of each of the kinds of food

  3. (answers may vary) We can solve the inhomogeneous equation by relaxing it to rational solutions (and then finding integer solutions). First, we need to solve the homogeneous equation 2x+3y+4z=02x + 3y + 4z = 02x+3y+4z=0. By making the assumption x=0x = 0x=0, y=4y = 4y=4, we get the solution z=−3z = -3z=−3, so [0,4,−3][0, 4, -3][0,4,−3] is a particular non-zero solution. By making the different assumption x=2x = 2x=2, y=0y = 0y=0, we get the solution z=−1z = -1z=−1, so [2,0,−1][2, 0, -1][2,0,−1] is a particular non-zero solution. Problem 3: True or False*

Classify each statement as True or False.

  1. All real vector spaces are also rational vector spaces.

  2. All rational vector spaces are also real vector spaces.

Solution

  1. This is true. Since the rational numbers are included in the real numbers, if we can multiply anything in VVV by any real number and stay in VVV, then we can also do that with rational numbers.

  2. This is false. A counterexample is Q\mathbf{Q}Q itself, which is a rational vector space but not a real vector space.
